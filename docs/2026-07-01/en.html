QVal: Cheaply Evaluating Dense Supervision Signals for Long-Horizon LLM Agents

Researchers have introduced QVal, an approach to evaluating dense supervision signals for long-horizon language model (LLM) agents, which operate over long periods where a single trajectory can contain hundreds or thousands of actions. This matters because, in these contexts, outcome-only rewards are too sparse and do not provide information about the quality of intermediate actions. QVal allows for the cheap and direct evaluation of supervision signal quality, without the need to integrate them into a training pipeline, which is common current practice. This facilitates the comparison of different dense supervision methods, which were previously difficult to compare due to the varying training setups required.

CoMet: Context and Multiplicity Decomposition for Multimodal Uncertainty Estimation

Researchers have proposed CoMet, a method for estimating uncertainty in multimodal large language models (MLLMs), a long-standing challenge in artificial intelligence that boils down to "knowing what you don't know". Uncertainty in MLLMs can arise from various sources and relationships between them, and CoMet addresses this by decomposing uncertainty into two terms: a context-specific term and a multiplicity-specific term, capturing the ambiguity induced by the given context and the uncertainty stemming from the variety of possible responses. This matters because uncertainty estimation is crucial for artificial intelligence models to be reliable and transparent. With CoMet, researchers aim to advance the resolution of this challenge, which is essential for building trust in AI systems.

Wat3R: Underwater 3D Geometry Learning without Annotations

Researchers have developed Wat3R, a new approach to learning 3D geometry in underwater environments without the need for annotations, which is a challenge due to light attenuation and scattering, as well as the lack of large-scale, high-quality 3D annotations. Wat3R uses a cross-domain semi-supervised learning framework that adapts feed-forward 3D reconstruction models from air to underwater scenes, eliminating the need for annotated underwater data. This is made possible by a teacher-student architecture that learns robust geometry representations merely from abundant unlabeled real underwater video footage. The development of Wat3R can be a significant step for underwater scene reconstruction, as it does not rely on massive annotations, which is impractical in underwater settings.

ZipDepth: Bringing Lightweight Zero-Shot Monocular Depth Anywhere, on Any Device

Researchers have introduced ZipDepth, a compact neural network for estimating depth from a single camera image, which can run on mobile and embedded devices due to its reduced size of 6.1 million parameters. This is notable because previous depth estimation models required significant computational resources, making them inaccessible to mobile platforms. ZipDepth combines an efficient encoder-decoder with knowledge distilled from a foundation model over a large multi-domain training set. As a result, ZipDepth achieves real-time processing rates, from server GPUs to power-constrained devices, making it a significant step forward in this field.

Geometry and Gradient-based Partitioning for Panoramic Outdoor Reconstruction

A new method for 3D reconstruction in large outdoor scenes has been proposed, aiming to reduce the costs of data acquisition and computation, which are high with the 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) technique. Using panoramic images with equirectangular projection (ERP) can decrease the capture effort due to their 360-degree field of view, but the resulting omnipresent visibility invalidates existing partitioning strategies that rely on local camera frustums. The proposed method, called PanoLOG, is a two-stage framework that leverages sky-sphere modeling and panoramic monocular depth supervision to obtain reliable geometry, addressing the limitations of current approaches.
